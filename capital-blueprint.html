A Ghanaian Entrepreneur's Guide to Securing Funding

According to business consultant Diane Akufo, the answer is not a shortage of capital — it is a shortage of readiness. This blueprint turns her complete roadmap into a step-by-step action checklist. Phase 01
Investor Readiness
Before you approach any investor, your legal and compliance foundation must be solid. This is your entry ticket into the funding ecosystem.
01 · Legal Structure
Register as a Limited Liability Company by Shares
A sole proprietorship merges you legally with your business — making you uninvestable. Only an LLC by shares allows you to issue equity to investors. This is non-negotiable and is your first step, not your last.
02 · Tax Compliance
Register with the Ghana Revenue Authority (GRA)
Investors verify your tax status during due diligence. If you are not GRA-registered with an active filing history, the conversation ends before a single slide is presented.
03 · Employee Compliance
Register your employees with SSNIT
Investors scrutinise your team section for compliance. SSNIT registration for all staff is evidence that you run a legitimate, professionally managed operation — not a side project.
04 · Filing History
Build a documented filing history with the government
A personal spreadsheet is not evidence — it is private and unverifiable. Your financial history must exist in government records. Start filing now. The longer your track record, the stronger your credibility.
05 · Business Banking
Open a dedicated business bank account
Your business is a separate legal entity — treat it like one. A dedicated account creates a clean, auditable financial paper trail. Investors will request statements. Make sure they tell the right story.
06 · Personal Separation
Pay yourself a formal salary — stop mixing personal and business funds
The moment investors find personal expenses running through a business account, trust collapses. Set a salary, stick to it, and treat the business as if you are its employee — not its personal bank account.
"Investors in Ghana rarely fund ideas alone. They need to see something in hand — compliance, traction, and a professional approach. The idea is just the starting point."
— Diane Akufo  ·  Konnected Minds Podcast
Phase 02
The Pitching Kit — 10 Deck Essentials
Your pitch deck is the visual summary of your entire business. Every one of these 10 components must be present. Missing even one signals to investors that your business is incomplete.
01 · Problem
Define the specific problem your business solves
Not an abstract global issue — a specific, researchable, on-the-ground problem in your target market. Back it with data from real conversations with real potential customers. Ideas generated in isolation are not investable.
02 · Solution
Show proof of your solution — not just a concept
Present a working MVP, a pilot, or a prototype. A solution without evidence is still just an idea. It must also demonstrate willingness to pay at your price point — the Ghanaian market is price-driven. Prove the math works.
03 · Market Size
Define your TAM, SAM, and Ghana-specific SOM
TAM = Total Addressable Market (global potential). SAM = Serviceable Market (who you can realistically reach). SOM = what you can actually capture given your finances, location, and resources. Being in a billion-dollar global industry means nothing if your Ghanaian SOM is 3,000 customers.
04 · Traction & Data
Show traction — this is your primary currency
Waitlists, active users, pre-orders, pilot results, or consistent sales. Traction is defined differently by industry — know what yours looks like and document it. If you have none, build it before you approach anyone with a chequebook.
05 · Competitor Analysis
Include a full competitor analysis — competitors are an advantage
Competitors prove the market already exists and has been validated. Zero competitors means the market is unproven and your cost to validate it will be enormous. Know who is in the space, what they charge, and exactly how you are differentiated.
06 · Team
Introduce your team and evidence their compliance
Investors fund people as much as ideas. Show relevant expertise, clear roles, and — critically — SSNIT registration for all employees. This signals that you run a structured, accountable organisation, not a loose arrangement.
07 · Financial Projections
Provide realistic financial forecasts — no inflated numbers
Inflated projections get exposed in due diligence. Understated ones signal you do not understand your own business. Build projections grounded in your actual SOM, cost structure, and growth rate. Update them as your business evolves. These must hold under scrutiny.
08 · Unit Economics
Know your CAC, your LTV, and the 3x Rule
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = what you spend to get one customer. LTV (Lifetime Value) = what that customer spends with you over time. Rule: if your LTV is less than 3x your CAC, your model is burning money on every customer. Investors will ask these numbers by heart. Know them.
09 · Market Research
Document validated, on-the-ground market research
Prove that real potential customers exist, that they have purchasing power, and that they are willing to pay at your price. Pre-orders, interviews, surveys, or pilot data all qualify. Assumptions and guesswork do not.
10 · Business Model
Demonstrate a reachable, achievable, and consistent business model
Show how your business generates consistent revenue. Investors want to see that you are building a viable operation — profitable, sustainable, solving a real problem — rather than chasing an unrealistic valuation in a market that cannot support it.
Know Your Target — Zebra vs. Unicorn
🦓 Zebra
  • Profitable and consistent
  • Solves real local problems
  • Sustainable cash flow
  • Realistic in Ghana's market
  • Attracts steady investment
🦄 Unicorn
  • $1B+ valuation goal
  • Rapid growth, exit strategy
  • Built for IPO or acquisition
  • Zero Ghanaian-owned in Ghana
  • Extremely high validation cost
Phase 03
Financial Management
Trust is built through numbers. These are the financial fundamentals every investor will test you on — get comfortable with all of them before you walk into any room.
01 · CAC
Calculate your Customer Acquisition Cost
Sum everything spent on marketing and sales in a given period. Divide by the number of customers acquired in that period. If you cannot calculate this confidently, you cannot defend your business model in a pitch.
02 · LTV
Calculate your Customer Lifetime Value
How much does a single customer spend with you over the entire duration of your relationship? Average order value × purchase frequency × average customer lifespan. Track this honestly. It tells you whether your business model works.
03 · The 3x Rule
Verify your LTV is at least 3x your CAC
If a customer does not spend at least three times what it cost to acquire them, you are losing money at scale. Fix this before pitching. Investors will catch it in due diligence if you do not catch it first.
04 · MoMo Records
Keep and organise your Mobile Money (MoMo) transaction reports
For informal-sector businesses, MoMo reports are your modern bank statement. They validate your financial activity and revenue history. Download, label, and store every month's records. These are increasingly accepted as formal financial documentation in Ghana.
05 · Bank Statements
Prepare clean, organised bank statements for due diligence
Investors will request these. Clean means: no personal expenses mixed in, no unexplained transactions, and no cash gaps that cannot be accounted for. Begin keeping your account clean now — even if you plan to pitch 12 months from today.
06 · Living Business Plan
Maintain a living business plan — update it as your business evolves
A business plan written once and never revised is a historical document, not a strategic tool. Update it when your market shifts, your model changes, or your financials move. It must reflect the business as it exists today — not the idea you had at launch.
"The type of person seeking the money is just as important as the business itself. Investors are not just funding ideas — they are funding people. Your conduct, professionalism, and communication are all part of the pitch."
— Diane Akufo  ·  Konnected Minds Podcast
Phase 04
Know What You're Building
Your business type determines which investors you target, what they expect from you, and how your pitch must be framed. Misalignment here wastes everyone's time.
01 · Business Type
Identify whether you are a Startup or a Traditional/SME
Startups are built for rapid growth and eventual exit. SMEs are built for legacy and consistent profitability. Both are valid paths. Both attract fundamentally different capital and investors. Know which category you are in before you pitch to anyone.
02 · Valuation Target
Decide: Are you building a Zebra or chasing a Unicorn?
Ghana has no Ghanaian-owned unicorns. The unicorns operating in Ghana are multinationals. For most entrepreneurs, the zebra model — consistent, profitable, solving local problems with real customers — is the realistic and fundable path. Choose it deliberately, not by default.
03 · Exit Strategy
If building a startup, define your exit strategy
Startup investors expect to see a return — through acquisition, IPO, or secondary sale. If you cannot answer how they get their money back, you are signalling that you are building a lifestyle business. Know your path to exit before the conversation happens.
04 · Local Context
Localise your model to Ghana's specific market dynamics
A trillion-dollar global market statistic in your pitch means nothing without Ghana-specific grounding. Calculate your SOM around local purchasing power, infrastructure, geography, and the competitive landscape. Investors here know the market. Use numbers that reflect it.
Phase 05
Funding Frameworks
Understand the instruments available before you enter any negotiation. Knowing what you are agreeing to is how you protect your business — and your equity.
Know Your Options
Traditional — Susu / Community / Apprenticeship
Trust-based models that still work for early-stage capital in the informal sector. Effective for small groups, difficult to scale beyond 20–30 people. A legitimate and accessible first source of startup funds for many Ghanaian entrepreneurs.
SAFE Agreement — Simple Agreement for Future Equity
An investor provides capital now. It converts to equity later when you raise a formal round. No interest payments, no loan repayment. The most accessible modern instrument — but the "simple" in its name is misleading. The term sheet still needs a lawyer.
Convertible Note
Structured as a loan with interest and a maturity date. Converts to equity during a follow-on funding round. If no round occurs before maturity, repayment may be required. Understand the timeline and interest terms before agreeing.
Equity Investment
You sell a percentage of your company in exchange for capital. Requires an LLC by shares structure. Investors own part of your business and expect a return. Scrutinise every percentage and clause — some investors have acquired majority control through contracts founders did not fully understand.
01 · Traditional Capital
Explore traditional funding mechanisms for early-stage capital
Susu networks, community lending circles, and apprenticeship-style capital can fund your first stages without requiring documentation. Use them to build traction and your first proof points — then use that evidence when approaching formal investors.
02 · SAFE vs. Convertible
Understand the practical difference between SAFE notes and Convertible Notes
SAFE: no interest, no repayment, converts to equity later. Convertible Note: structured as debt with a clock — if you do not raise a follow-on round before it matures, you may owe money you do not have. Know which instrument you are being offered before you discuss terms.
03 · Equity Limits
Decide your equity limits before entering any negotiation
Determine the maximum percentage of your business you are willing to give away before you sit down with any investor. Some entrepreneurs have lost majority control through contracts they did not fully understand. Enter negotiations with a firm, pre-decided number.
04 · Valuation Awareness
Understand how your business will be valued
Investors use methods like discounted cash flow and industry comparables to determine what your business is worth. Know your numbers well enough to understand — and challenge, if necessary — how a valuation is arrived at. Your equity stake depends on it.
05 · Legal Review
Engage a lawyer before signing any term sheet
Some term sheets contain clauses that allow investors to assume majority control if a founder attempts to exit or misses specific performance targets. A lawyer is not an optional expense at this stage — it is the minimum protection for everything you have built. Do not skip it.
Phase 06
Finding Investors
Now that you are prepared, here is exactly where to go — and precisely how to show up when you get there.
01 · LinkedIn
Use LinkedIn with directness — not "hi hello"
One clear, specific message. State who you are, what your business does, your traction, and what you are asking for. Investors receive hundreds of vague messages. Specificity and brevity get replies. Write your message once, refine it until it is perfect, then send it.
02 · Events & Conferences
Attend investor networking events — but arrive prepared, not just present
The event gets you into the room. Your preparation is what closes the deal. Have your pitch deck on your phone, your core numbers memorised, and your business plan updated before you walk through the door. Showing up unprepared wastes an opportunity that someone else will take.
03 · Incubators
Apply to structured incubator programs like The Big 10
Incubators such as The Big 10 — created by Diane Akufo — are designed to train entrepreneurs to investor-readiness and then connect qualified participants with capital through the facilitator's network. Apply correctly, follow every instruction precisely, and treat every stage of the process as part of the evaluation.

05 · Professional Conduct
Treat every investor interaction as part of the pitch
Your email tone, punctuality, how you dress, and the environment you take calls from — all of it communicates whether you are someone an investor can trust with their capital. The pitch does not start when you open the deck. It starts with the first message you send.

Phase 07
Common Pitfalls
These are the mistakes that end investor conversations before a single slide is shown. Every one of them is avoidable. Know them. Eliminate them entirely.
❌
Applying to programs incorrectly
Of over 100,000 people reached during The Big 10 launch, only 23 applied correctly via the provided link. Read every instruction. Follow every step. Careless applications disqualify you before you are ever evaluated on merit.
❌
Taking investor calls from unprofessional environments
Candidates for The Big 10 took interviews from funerals and rooms filled with friends, laughter, and music. The environment you choose for an investor interaction communicates your standards — and investors draw immediate conclusions from it.
❌
Dressing inappropriately for investor meetings
Slippers and shorts to a formal investor meeting communicate that you are not treating the opportunity seriously. Dress to match the level of capital you are asking for. First impressions in high-stakes environments are rarely corrected.
❌
Sending vague or non-specific messages to investors
The "hi hi hello hello" approach is endemic. Every vague message is a wasted touch point. One sentence should communicate who you are, what you do, what stage you are at, and what you need. Practice that sentence until it is perfect.
❌
Mixing personal and business finances
Investors perform financial due diligence. The moment personal expenses appear in a business account, trust collapses and the deal typically ends. Separate your finances permanently — not before the pitch, but from the day you start.
❌
Using inflated or unrealistic financial projections
Inflated projections impress nobody who knows finance. They signal dishonesty or a fundamental misunderstanding of your business during due diligence. Build projections grounded in your SOM and actual data. Investors will model your numbers. Make sure they survive scrutiny.
❌
Chasing unicorn status in a zebra market
Pursuing a $1B valuation in a market that cannot support it is not bold ambition — it is strategic misalignment. Understand Ghana's economic dynamics and build a business that is achievable, profitable, and scalable within that context first. Growth happens in stages.
❌
Signing a term sheet without legal review
Some term sheets contain clauses allowing investors to assume majority control if founders attempt to exit or miss specific targets. These clauses are written in language that requires expertise to identify. Never sign without a lawyer reviewing every line. No exceptions.

The Big 10 incubator programme was designed to help 10 entrepreneurs raise 100,000 Ghana Cedis each. Due to incorrect applications and unprofessional conduct during interviews, only 3 qualified participants were found from over 100,000 people reached. The opportunity existed. The readiness did not. Do not let that be your story.
