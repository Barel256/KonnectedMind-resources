Start Anyway.

The Zero-Capital Business Checklist — inspired by how Samuel built two bakeries from 600 GHC

Samuel didn't wait for funding, a business plan, or the right conditions. He started with 200 GHC of his own money and a 400 GHC mobile loan. This checklist pulls the exact moves he made — so you can apply them this week, not someday.

The Checklist — 8 Actions
01

Stop waiting for big money

Write down the smallest version of your idea that could generate even one sale this week. Samuel didn't buy a full bag of flour — he bought only what he needed to begin. What is your minimum starting point?

02

Research the problem, not the product

Most people research what to sell. Samuel researched who is suffering — diabetics needed bread that wouldn't spike their glucose. Find a group of people with a real problem and work backwards from there.

03

Look at the "dirty" jobs everyone is avoiding

Graduates skip the roles that feel beneath them. That's exactly where the money is sitting uncollected. Write down three jobs in your area that nobody your age wants to do. Those are your fastest opportunities.

04

Use social media to educate, not advertise

Samuel used Instagram and TikTok Live to teach — not to run ads. One TikTok Live reached 5,000 people in under 40 minutes. Pick one platform and teach what you know about your product this week.

05

Use your network to cut costs

Samuel used his mother's market knowledge to find the cheapest raw materials in Kaneshi. Who in your current circle knows where to source cheaper, faster, or smarter? Ask before you spend.

06

Become the connection man

No resources? No problem. Find someone with a problem and someone with the solution — take a margin for making the introduction. This is a real business model, not a shortcut.

07

Learn one handywork skill — now, while you can

Plumbing, welding, sewing, car repair. Samuel says these skills print money after graduation when everyone else is waiting for job offers. Pick one. Start learning it this month.

08

Reinvest before you celebrate

When your first money comes in, put it back into the business — better raw materials, a course, a tool. The flashy things come later. The track record you build now is what attracts real investors.
